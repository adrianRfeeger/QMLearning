# 1. The Quantum World

Classical mechanics—the physics of Isaac Newton and James Clerk Maxwell—is incredibly successful at describing the everyday world. It tells us how planets orbit the sun, how cars accelerate, and how light propagates through a vacuum. 

However, at the turn of the 20th century, physicists began probing the very small: atoms, electrons, and photons of light. To their astonishment, the rules of classical mechanics broke down. The universe at the microscopic scale behaves in ways that defy our everyday intuition.

## 1.1 The Failure of Classical Physics

There were a few key mysteries that classical physics could not explain:

1. **The Ultraviolet Catastrophe**: Classical theory predicted that a blackbody object at thermal equilibrium would emit infinite energy at high frequencies (ultraviolet light), which is physically impossible. Max Planck solved this in 1900 by proposing that energy is quantized—it can only be emitted or absorbed in discrete "chunks" called quanta.
2. **The Photoelectric Effect**: When light shines on a metal, electrons can be ejected. However, the energy of the ejected electrons depends only on the *frequency* (color) of the light, not its *intensity* (brightness). Albert Einstein explained this in 1905 by proposing that light itself consists of particles called **photons**.
3. **Atomic Spectra**: Atoms only emit and absorb light at very specific, discrete frequencies. Classical mechanics predicted that an orbiting electron would continuously radiate energy and spiral into the nucleus. Niels Bohr proposed a model where electrons inhabit discrete, quantized orbits.

## 1.2 Wave-Particle Duality

One of the most mind-bending concepts in quantum mechanics is **wave-particle duality**. 

In the classical world, things are either particles (like baseballs) or waves (like ripples in a pond). A particle is localized at a specific point in space, while a wave is spread out. 

In the quantum world, entities like electrons and photons exhibit both particle-like and wave-like properties depending on how they are measured.

### The Double-Slit Experiment
If you fire particles (like tiny paintballs) through a barrier with two slits, they form two distinct bands on a screen behind the barrier. 
If you shine a wave (like a water wave) through the two slits, the waves emanating from the two slits interfere with each other, creating an **interference pattern** of many alternating bright and dark bands on the screen.

When physicists fired individual electrons (which we think of as particles) through the double slits one by one, an astonishing thing happened. Over time, the individual particle hits built up an interference pattern! Even though the electrons were fired one at a time, they somehow interfered like a wave.

This implies that a quantum particle does not have a single, definitive trajectory. Instead, it exists in a **superposition** of possibilities until it is measured.

## 1.3 What is a Quantum State?

In classical physics, if you know the exact position and momentum of a particle, you know everything about its state, and you can predict its future perfectly. 

In quantum mechanics, a system is described by a **quantum state**, often represented by a wave function (denoted by the Greek letter Psi, $\Psi$). The wave function doesn't tell you exactly where a particle is. Instead, it gives you the *probability* of finding the particle in a certain location or with a certain property when you measure it.

We'll dive into the mathematics of how to work with these probabilities in the next chapter.
